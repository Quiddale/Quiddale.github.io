DESIGN PROJECTS

Drone Orchestra

I worked as part of small team responsible for building a drone orchestra for a John Cale concert. We designed, built and programmed a swarm of DGI drones to buzz around the auditorium, playing music over the heads of the audience like android wasps. A cube draped in tube lights… part jellyfish, part Orac from Blake’s 7… hovers menacingly before the balcony. "LOOP60Hz: Transmissions from the Drone Orchestra was a collaboration between airborne architecture and music," explains Cale, a multi-instrumentalist, and one of the founding members of the Velvet Underground.

A FICTIONAL CITY

Future Perfect is an architectural design project for a fictional, future city. The idea I wanted to realise is what would happen if a think-tank of scientists, technologists, designers, artists and science-fiction authors all collaborated to develop a new city. What would the city itself be like? And what of the landscape that surrounds it? What stories would it contain?

A WORLD ADRIFT

For this project we travelled east to Hong Kong and set off on board a mega container ship to Yiwu in the manufacturing heart of Southern China. We traced the shipping lanes and cargo routes, the watery pathways of consumerism, through the China Seas, exploring the criss-crossed networks that connect China’s monumental manufacturing output with all the far-flung places of the world.

WHO SAID DRAWING SHOULD BE LIKE WATCHING TV

In this project I imagined The Tinker Network, a shared virtual space created where the physical world and the digital converge, applying augmented reality and the internet. Within this context I re-imagined the classroom using autonomous drones and google cardboard and considered what possibilities this could open up for child’s education. How might a child choose to digitally augment her world? What or who would guide her through it? How might her observations of the real world feed back into her virtual classroom?

Architecture as Protest

This project began with a projection into the future in which water was no longer free, and lead to the design of a human prosthesis. A wearable augmentation to filter urine and improve the body’s efficiency to use and re-use water. Moving beyond the human scale I found a loophole in the way global water sources are managed – no one can own the water falling in a waterfall.

About

That said, welcome to my page. I discovered coding in January 2017 so compared to the rest of the community I’m somewhat of a noob, but I’m learning. On June 26th 2017 I took my first step into a larger world when I started the Web Development Immersive course at General Assembly in London, Immersive being the optimum word. 12 gruelling weeks, all day, everyday (weekends too) of languages, command lines, editors, graphics, databases, tears, rapidly greying hairs and enough tea and coffee to sink a ship, all designed to give aspiring developers as idea of the breath of material used in the world of development.

Hi and welcome to my page. I trained as an architect and I’ve always had a keen interest in coding, development and in digital design. This year, I decided to change path and develop my digital skill-set because I wanted to open up the number of worlds and the types of problems to which I can apply my design and creative problem-solving skills. So compared to many in the community I’m something of a noob. But I’m learning fast. In June I took a big step forward when I started the Web Development Immersive course at General Assembly in London. And immersive really is the operative word. Twelve fantastic, challenging weeks, all day, every day (weekends too) dedicated to languages, command lines, editors, graphics, databases, rapidly greying hairs and enough tea and coffee to sink a battle ship. All to give aspiring developers like me a rapid understanding of the wonderful world of web development. I loved every minute of it.

To design, to think broadly, to invent, to make, to test, to fail, to learn, to question and debate, to stretch a definition and to solve problems beautifully. This is what I love to do. Two people in particular inspire me. Seymour Papert, the mathematician, computer scientist, educational theorist and the inventor of Lego MindStorm. His work applying digital technology to help children think, learn and solve problems through play has inspired some of my own projects. And the futurist, Neal Stephenson. In Snow Crash, Stephenson introduces us to the idea of the metaverse. The prefix “meta” meaning “beyond” with “universe” and is now used to describe the future of the internet, a place comprised of shared, virtual spaces in a virtual universe. I really want to help build the metaverse,

So here I am, out the other side, a little older, slightly wiser, and with a far better software development skills than I had at the beginning of the year. Ofcourse I’m still green, but I love that working in this field requires life-long learning. It’s a core part of who I am.

I also love cycling and to my family’s delight am working my way through The Food Lab: Better Home Cooking Through Science by J. Kenji Lopez-Alt. I’m not sure that swapping out one science for another is a recommended approach to relaxation, but cooking certainly makes a lovely break from coding.
